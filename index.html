Default Case Study Guide: Smart IoT Lock

Context: SecuTech is building a premium Smart IoT Lock. Key customer requirements are centered around reliability, battery lifespan, ease of installation, and mobile synchronization. The QFD helps align these WHATs to key design specifications (HOWs).

Step 1 (WHATs): Input customer voices like "Long battery life" (Importance: 5) and "Instant mobile sync" (Importance: 4).
Step 2 (HOWs): Map engineering design metrics like "Standby current draw" (mA), "Bluetooth chip TX power" (dBm), and "Enclosure sealing level" (IP rating).
Step 3 (Relationships): Strong mapping (9) is established between "Long battery life" and "Standby current draw".
Step 4 (Roof Correlation): Conflict is highlighted between "Bluetooth chip TX power" (wants high power for signal) and "Standby current draw" (wants low power for battery) - marked as a strong negative (--) trade-off.
Step 5 (Market Benchmark): SecuTech compares its lock against Competitor A & B. Competitor B leads on mobile sync speed.
Step 6 (Technical Targets): Design target direction is set (e.g. minimize standby current to < 0.05 mA).

What is House of Quality?

The House of Quality is the core matrix of **Quality Function Deployment (QFD)**. Developed in Japan by Yoji Akao in 1966, it acts as a conceptual map linking customer voices (WHATs) directly to manufacturing and engineering parameters (HOWs).

By resolving conflicts (the Roof), analyzing competitors, and scoring priorities, designers can focus resources on design specs that maximize customer satisfaction.

Priority Formulas

Engineering Absolute Importance Score:

\[W_{abs, j} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} (R_{i,j} \times I_i)\]

Where \(R_{i,j}\) is relationship weight (9, 3, 1) and \(I_i\) is customer importance (1–5).

Relative Engineering Weight (%):

\[W_{rel, j} = \frac{W_{abs, j}}{\sum_{k} W_{abs, k}} \times 100\%\]

Step 3: Inter-Relationship Matrix

Quantify how strongly each Technical Spec (HOW) impacts each Customer Need (WHAT). Select (9) for Strong, (3) for Medium, (1) for Weak, or blank for None.

Step 4: Engineering Spec Correlations (The Roof)

Map correlations between different Technical Specs (HOWs) to resolve design conflicts. If improving Spec A degrades Spec B, mark as Negative. If it aids, mark as Positive.

Step 5: Market Performance Benchmarking

Rate customer perception of performance (1–5 Scale) for your company and core competitors on each Customer Requirement.

Step 6: Engineering Targets & Competitor Tech Benchmark

Establish target values and list competitors' technical scores for each engineering specification.
